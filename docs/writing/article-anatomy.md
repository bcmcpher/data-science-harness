# Anatomy of a primary research article

Which sections a research paper contains, what each is *for*, how each is structured
internally, and the order in which to write them. Synthesized from Schwabe et al. (2016),
Kallestinova (2011), Carandini (2022), Fried (2021), and the Miami U LibGuide. For reviews and
meta-analyses, see [`reviews-and-meta-analyses.md`](reviews-and-meta-analyses.md); for
paragraph/sentence craft used *within* every section, see [`prose-craft.md`](prose-craft.md).

## Write in this order (not top-to-bottom)

Most guides converge on drafting from the most concrete outward (Miami U; Schwabe et al.):

1. **Figures first** — draft the figures as the "storyboard" of the paper *before* the prose is
   finished. They reveal gaps in the story and give co-authors something concrete to align on
   (Schwabe et al.).
2. **Methods** → 3. **Results** (describe what the figures show) → 4. **Introduction** (now you
   know exactly what story you are setting up) → 5. **Discussion** → 6. **Conclusion** →
   7. **Abstract** and **Title** (last — they distill a finished paper) → 8. **References**.

A working title and a figure storyboard early keep multi-author teams focused. Refine the title
and abstract only once the body is stable.

## The shape: hourglass / funnel

The paper is an **hourglass** (Fried 2021): it opens broad (Introduction connects to the
reader's interests), narrows to a tight focus (the study itself — Methods/Results), then widens
again (Discussion/Conclusion reconnect to the larger context). The Introduction is
**funnel-shaped** — general → specific — and the Discussion runs the **opposite logic**,
specific → general (Schwabe et al.).

## The three communicative tasks (Kazdin 2013)

Every section is doing one or more of three jobs; weak papers do only the first (Kazdin 2013):
- **Description** — report the details (participants, measures, procedures, results) fully enough
  to evaluate and replicate. The easy part, yet basic details are often omitted.
- **Explanation** — give the **rationale** for each decision (why this sample, design, measure,
  analysis). Anticipate and **pre-empt** the reader's "why did you do it that way?" at every
  decision point.
- **Contextualization** — place the study in the knowledge base and make the case for why it
  matters. "First study of X" is not, by itself, a reason; convey the specific gap this fills.

Kazdin's **Table 10.1** ("Major questions to guide journal article preparation") turns this into
a section-by-section list of questions every section should answer — reused as the backbone of the
`draft-manuscript` skill (see [`SPEC.md`](SPEC.md)).

## Title

- One specific, brief sentence; **≤ ~15 words** (Schwabe et al.). Include the organism/population
  where relevant (Miami U).
- Prefer **active voice** and state the main message/finding — it reads more strongly (Schwabe
  et al.). Good: "Neurons in medial prefrontal cortex signal memory for fear extinction."
- **Avoid** empty frames: "A study of…", "Effects of…", "Examining the…", and subtitles.
- Prefer **substantive/content words over filler** ("preliminary findings," "implications," "new
  findings" say little and hurt indexing) and **avoid yes/no-question titles** — findings are
  usually "both yes and no under different conditions" (Kazdin 2013).
- The title is one of the two things (with the abstract) by which readers decide to read on —
  it does real work.

## Abstract

- A **single paragraph** condensing the entire study (Miami U): background → aim/hypothesis →
  methods → key findings → implications.
- Written **last**; obey the journal word limit (often 100–250 words), which forces concision.
- Design it so the paper is understandable from **title + abstract + figures** alone
  (Carandini). No citations, no undefined abbreviations.
- Make every statement **substantive, not vacuous** — replace "implications were discussed" or
  "future directions were suggested" with the *specific* implications/directions (Kazdin 2013).
  For many readers the abstract is the only part they read.

## Introduction

**Purpose:** state the scientific problem, give just enough background, and make the reader care —
then land the specific question(s) and hypothesis.

**Structure — the CARS three moves** (Kallestinova 2011):
1. **Establish the territory** — show the general area matters (broad, but not a textbook review).
2. **Establish a niche** — name the gap, tension, or unresolved question in prior work.
3. **Occupy the niche** — state your purpose, specific questions, hypotheses, and contribution.

**Key techniques:**
- Open with a **hook** — a startling fact/statistic, a provocative question, a paradox, or a
  crisp framing sentence (Fried 2021). Avoid a slow, generic first sentence.
- **Pose specific questions** — "these questions are the pillars of the paper" (Carandini). Move
  from vague intuition to explicit questions the rest of the paper answers.
- **Develop the hypothesis step by step** so the reader "sees it coming on the horizon" before
  you state it; arrange the cited literature to build toward it (Schwabe et al.).
- Answer **"so what?"** explicitly — say why the question matters rather than asserting it is
  "interesting" or "important" (Carandini; Fried 2021).
- Prefer **original papers over reviews** when crediting findings (Schwabe et al.).
- Keep it tight — many journals cap the Introduction near **500 words** (Schwabe et al.). It may
  close with a one-sentence preview of the approach and main result.

## Methods (Materials and Methods)

**Purpose:** let a competent peer **evaluate and reproduce** the work.

- Provide enough detail to reproduce; do not omit critical parameters (samples, devices,
  settings, timings, software versions) (Kallestinova 2011).
- Organize by field convention: cognitive work often runs chronologically; cellular/molecular
  work often by method type. Usually **past tense**.
- **Keep point of view consistent within a paragraph** — do not flip between passive and active
  voice mid-paragraph (Kallestinova 2011). Passive voice is common here; if using "we," vary
  sentence openings to avoid a "we did… we did…" drone.
- Usually **few or no citations**, except to reference an established method (Miami U).
- A short Methods with a detailed **supplement** is acceptable in many venues (Schwabe et al.).
  Do not write "see Methods" in Results — assume the reader knows they are there (Carandini).

## Results

**Purpose:** present the findings — **no interpretation** (Miami U).

- Open with a brief **roadmap** of how the results are organized; use subsections whose titles
  match their figures (Carandini).
- **Link statistics to meaning** — don't string bare test values together. Tell the reader what
  the numbers *mean* as you report them (Schwabe et al.):
  - Weak: "The ANOVA showed a main effect of condition (F… P…) and a condition × emotion
    interaction (F… P…)."
  - Better: "Stress impaired memory overall (F… P…), and most so for emotionally arousing items
    (condition × emotion: F… P…)."
- Present **key findings in more than one sentence and in a figure** so they are not overlooked;
  put exact/less-important statistics in **tables** (Schwabe et al.).
- Report only detail essential to understanding the finding; distinguish results from raw data
  (Kallestinova 2011). Avoid empty intensifiers ("clearly," "obviously").
- Every figure/table panel must be referenced in the text.

### Figures (the storyboard)

- Figures carry the story and let readers form their own conclusions; invest in them (Schwabe
  et al.). A skimming reader should get the paper from the figures alone.
- **Minimize ink** (Carandini, after Tufte): remove boxes, grids, dark backgrounds, and
  decorative elements that obscure the data.
- Progress from **exemplar → population** (individual example, then the summary) (Carandini).
- Be **consistent and accessible**: one color scheme, colorblind-safe palettes, aligned panels,
  consistent fonts; match colormap dimensionality to the data.
- Reference figures **parenthetically** to keep the science the subject: "The effect reversed
  with age (Fig. 3)," not "Figure 3 shows that…" (Carandini).
- Build at final print size; never manipulate images in ways that violate data integrity; move
  numbers, CIs, and p-values into tables/legends (Schwabe et al.; Carandini).

## Discussion

**Purpose:** answer the questions posed in the Introduction, interpret the findings, and place
them in the broader field. Runs the **opposite logic to the Introduction** (specific → general)
(Schwabe et al.).

**Structure — three moves** (Kallestinova 2011; Schwabe et al.):
1. **Open** by restating the study's objective and **summarizing the main findings** in the first
   paragraph (e.g., "Our results suggest…"). Draw **conclusions**, not just restatements
   (Carandini).
2. **Contextualize** — discuss each key finding, beginning with the most important; relate to and
   contrast with prior work; address alternative interpretations; explain discrepancies and
   unexpected results.
3. **Close** — a final paragraph on broader implications and significance; note limitations
   briefly and **without an apologetic tone** (Schwabe et al.).

Guard the take-home: the story should be understandable from **Introduction + Results** alone
(Carandini). Don't over-reiterate the main finding, but don't let it get buried either.

## Conclusion

- A brief final section that **is not a mere summary — it is the final pitch** for the main idea
  (Fried 2021): remind the reader of the key point, **return to the opening hook**, widen the
  lens to the larger context, and state the take-away plus future directions.
- Introduce **no new concepts or results** here.

## References

- Include **every source cited in the text, and none that you didn't cite** (Miami U).
- Use a **citation manager** — never hand-enter references (Carandini).

## Beyond the manuscript: reporting standards, journal choice, cover letter & revision

(Getting the article out. For the *reviewer's* side of this process, see
[`reviewing-manuscripts.md`](reviewing-manuscripts.md).)

- **Reporting standards:** follow the applicable community standard so the report is complete —
  **CONSORT** (randomized trials), **TREND** (non-randomized), **APA JARS**, and discipline
  equivalents (Kazdin 2013); for reviews/meta-analyses see **PRISMA/MOOSE** in
  [`reviews-and-meta-analyses.md`](reviews-and-meta-analyses.md). These double as pre-writing
  checklists, not just formatting rules.
- **Selecting a journal:** match topic/scope, audience, prestige, and likelihood of acceptance;
  a good heuristic is which journals your own **reference list** cites most (Kazdin 2013). Avoid
  low-quality "hustle" outlets that accept nearly everything.
- **Cover letter:** a persuasive one-page business letter to the editor — title, a 1–2 paragraph
  statement of the main findings and their relevance in accessible language, why the paper fits
  the journal's scope; suggest suitable reviewers via the submission system; include required
  ethics statements. Be specific, not "our findings are novel and important" (Schwabe et al.).
- **Revision / rebuttal:** expect revision; respond to **every** reviewer comment separately and
  specifically in a numbered response letter; mark changes with page/line; treat reviewers as
  partners; stay factual and polite even with harsh reviews; where you disagree, argue the point
  respectfully rather than silently ignoring it (Schwabe et al.).

## Structural review checklist

- [ ] Can the paper be followed from **title + abstract + figures + topic sentences** alone?
- [ ] **Title** ≤ ~15 words, specific, active voice, states the message; no empty frame.
- [ ] **Introduction** funnels general→specific; hooks; poses explicit questions; builds the
      hypothesis step by step; answers "so what?"; ~≤500 words.
- [ ] **Methods** reproducible; consistent voice within paragraphs; correct tense.
- [ ] **Results** interpret nothing; stats linked to meaning; key findings in text + figure;
      exact stats in tables; every panel cited.
- [ ] **Figures** minimize ink, consistent, accessible, exemplar→population, referenced in parens.
- [ ] **Discussion** opens with objective + main findings; contextualizes most-important-first;
      addresses alternatives + limitations (non-apologetic); ends on implications.
- [ ] **Conclusion** is a pitch, returns to the hook, adds no new content.
- [ ] **References** complete and manager-generated; every citation used.
