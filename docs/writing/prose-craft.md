# Prose craft: paragraphs, sentences, and revision

How to build the sub-structure *inside* every section — paragraphs, sentences, word choice —
and how to revise. Synthesized from Carandini (2022), Kallestinova (2011), Fried (2021), and
Baumeister (2013). Use alongside [`article-anatomy.md`](article-anatomy.md).

## Paragraphs

**One paragraph = one point.** Each paragraph makes a single distinct point, summarized in its
**opening topic sentence** (Carandini). A rushed reader should be able to read only the topic
sentences and get the paper's argument; if a paragraph resists one-sentence summary, it needs
reorganizing (Carandini).

**The four-part paragraph** (Fried 2021):
1. **Topic sentence** — states the paragraph's point and connects back to the thesis/question.
2. **Evidence** — data of your own, or others' findings.
3. **Analysis** — never present evidence without interpreting it; tell the reader what it *means*
   for your point. (Evidence and analysis often share a sentence.)
4. **Transition** — the last sentence sets up the next paragraph; a short cue ("In contrast,"
   "Similarly,") links ideas. Don't overuse transition words.

**Sequencing.** Order topic sentences so that, read in series, they form a coherent summary of
the section. At the start of each paragraph, check how it relates to the previous one.

**Do not start a paragraph with a name** (Baumeister 2013). Opening with "Smith (2019)…" neglects
the transition and hides how the paragraph fits the argument. Use the first or last sentence to
connect to your take-home message instead. (Especially common — and especially harmful — in
literature reviews written one-paper-per-paragraph.)

## Sentences

**Old information first, new information last.** Begin a sentence with familiar/old material and
put the new material at the **end**, where readers expect the emphasis (Carandini, after Gopen &
Swan, *The Science of Scientific Writing*, 1990). Chaining "new-at-the-end → old-at-the-start of
the next sentence" is what makes prose flow.

**Voice and point of view:**
- Prefer **active voice** and, in the Discussion, first person ("we argue…") to convey confidence
  — unless the actor is irrelevant (Kallestinova 2011; Carandini). Methods is the usual exception
  (passive is common there).
- **Keep point of view consistent within a paragraph** — don't flip active/passive mid-paragraph
  (Kallestinova 2011).
- Replace weak linking-verb constructions with strong verbs: "A depends on B," not "A is
  dependent on B" (Carandini). Replace **nominalizations** with verbs: "we argue that…," not "we
  provide an argument that…" (Kallestinova 2011).

**Concision — omit needless words** (Carandini):
- Delete any word whose removal doesn't change the meaning.
- Cut reflexive fillers and intensifiers: "respectively," "recent," "very," "clearly,"
  "obviously," "quite," "essentially" (Carandini; Kallestinova 2011).
- Choose reasonable numeric precision (61%, not 61.37%) and keep decimal places consistent.

**Terminology — name it once, then never vary it** (Carandini): once you name a thing, use
exactly those words every time. Elegant-variation synonyms make readers wonder whether you mean
something different. Use American English for international accessibility.

**A note for reviews** (Baumeister 2013): keep a sharp distinction between what someone
*claimed/theorized* and what someone *showed/proved with data*. The citation format is identical
for both, so the writer must make the difference explicit ("X speculated that…" vs. "X showed,
in a controlled study, that…").

## Revision

Revise in **two passes at two scales** (Kallestinova 2011); expect 5–7 drafts to be normal
(Schwabe et al. echo the "revise many times" rule).

**Macrostructure pass** (organization, content, logic — do this first):
- Work from an updated outline; view the whole paper and check idea flow and logical order.
- Review section by section (~5 pages at a time); ignore wording — only structure and argument.

**Microstructure pass** (words, sentences, grammar):
- **Read aloud** (or record, or have a colleague read) — fluency breaks become audible.
- Keep a personal **error list** and use find-in-document to hunt your known tics (intensifiers,
  "there is/are," nominalizations).
- Print double-spaced at large font; mask surrounding text to focus on small chunks.

**Feedback:** get input from non-specialists; listen without defending; set a submission deadline
to stop endless revision (Kallestinova 2011). For non-native English writers, a native-speaker
read markedly helps the editor/reviewer experience (Schwabe et al.).

## Prose review checklist

- [ ] Every paragraph makes **one point**, stated in its **topic sentence**; topic sentences read
      as a summary of the section.
- [ ] Paragraphs follow **topic → evidence → analysis → transition**; no evidence left
      uninterpreted.
- [ ] No paragraph **starts with a name**.
- [ ] Sentences run **old → new** information; consecutive sentences chain.
- [ ] **Active voice** default (Methods excepted); point of view consistent within paragraphs.
- [ ] Nominalizations and weak linking verbs replaced with strong verbs.
- [ ] Needless words, fillers, and intensifiers cut; numeric precision reasonable and consistent.
- [ ] **One term per concept**, used consistently throughout.
- [ ] (Reviews) claim-vs-proof distinction is explicit for every cited source.
- [ ] Revised at **macro** (structure) then **micro** (line) scale; read aloud at least once.
