# Reviewing manuscript text

Guidance for the **review** half of the manuscript sub-agent — critiquing a draft (your own,
a colleague's, or a peer-review assignment) and writing the review. Distilled from Drotar, Wu &
Rohan (2013, *Portable Mentor* ch. 11), Kazdin (2013, ch. 10), and Schwabe et al. (2016). For
what "good" looks like in the text being reviewed, use the checklists in
[`article-anatomy.md`](article-anatomy.md), [`prose-craft.md`](prose-craft.md), and
[`reviews-and-meta-analyses.md`](reviews-and-meta-analyses.md).

## Four qualities of an effective review
Clarity, specificity, constructiveness, thoroughness (Hyman 1995, via Drotar et al. 2013). A
review should inform the editor and the author about the primary strengths and weaknesses in a
**focused** way.

## Focus on the major points, not the nits
- Center the critique on **scientific significance/contribution, design & methods, statistical
  analysis, and clarity/consistency of reporting** (Drotar et al. 2013; Kazdin's reviewer
  criteria below).
- **Distinguish major points** (that limit the significance or validity of the work) from
  **minor points** (editorial/formatting). Do not enumerate every grammatical or style slip —
  copy editors handle those; note globally ("there were several typographical/APA-style lapses")
  and move on (Drotar et al. 2013).
- The core judgment (Kazdin 2013): does the manuscript address a **relevant, significant
  question**? Are **design and methodology appropriate**? Are the **results suitably analyzed**?
  Do the **interpretations follow** from the design and findings? Does it yield an
  **incremental** contribution to what is known?

## Structure the review like a manuscript
- **Organize by manuscript section** — Abstract, Introduction, Methods, Results, Discussion —
  then key points (Drotar et al. 2013).
- **Number every specific point** so the author can respond point-by-point and the editor can
  track the response (Drotar et al. 2013).
- Keep it **concise: ~1–2 single-spaced pages** is an effective length; if your list runs much
  longer, prioritize and summarize the key points.
- For each key point, **give the rationale** and, where possible, an **explicit suggestion** for
  how to address it (and a reference if relevant) — clarity of rationale is what lets authors and
  editors act on the critique.

## Tone and stance
- **Honest yet balanced** — appraise strengths and weaknesses; be candid about problems.
- **Constructive and tactful** — critique is easier to act on when the tone is fair; proof your
  own review for clarity, specificity, and tone before submitting (Drotar et al. 2013).
- **Judge revision potential, not perfection.** Almost any manuscript *could* be improved; the
  question is whether a revision is **likely** to yield an important contribution. Distinguish
  **correctable** problems from **non-correctable** ones (fatal design/measurement flaws)
  (Drotar et al. 2013).

## Ethics of reviewing (Drotar et al. 2013; Palermo 2010)
- **Declare conflicts of interest** (close/active collaborators, same institution, rivals); if
  you cannot review impartially, decline and tell the editor.
- **Confidentiality** — the manuscript and your review are privileged; do not circulate or
  discuss them (mentored review is the one exception); **destroy/delete** the manuscript after
  the decision.
- **Comments to the editor** (not seen by authors) should be used **sparingly** and stay
  consistent with the feedback given to authors; refrain from an explicit accept/reject verdict
  in the author-facing text unless the journal asks.
- **Timeliness** — meet the deadline; if you cannot, decline early so the editor can reassign.

## Section-by-section critique checklist
(Adapted from Drotar et al. 2013, Table 11.2 — the reviewer counterpart to the author checklists.)

**Introduction**
- [ ] Is the purpose/research question specified, with specific hypotheses?
- [ ] Is the relevance and scientific/clinical significance vs. prior work articulated?
- [ ] Is a guiding theoretical/conceptual framework evident?

**Methods**
- [ ] Clear study design; if part of a larger dataset, is that relationship and prior
      publication derived from it stated?
- [ ] Follows relevant reporting standards (CONSORT/APA JARS as applicable); ethics detailed.
- [ ] Participants, non-participants (with refusal reasons), and attrition described.
- [ ] Sampling method, procedures (setting, timing, QC/training), and measures (reliability,
      validity for the current study) described.
- [ ] Analytic plan, sample size, and power addressed.

**Results**
- [ ] Follows data-presentation standards; overview provided; results tied to the study's
      questions/hypotheses.
- [ ] Assumptions for statistical tests reported; **effect sizes and confidence intervals**
      reported; missing data described.
- [ ] Text, tables, and figures integrated and not redundant; results succinct; clinical
      significance addressed where relevant.

**Discussion**
- [ ] Organized around key findings (not a re-list of results); findings synthesized against
      the literature framed in the Introduction.
- [ ] Novel contribution and significance articulated; limitations and threats to validity
      (internal/external) and their implications addressed.
- [ ] Future-research and (where relevant) clinical/applied implications described.

**For a review or meta-analysis under review:** additionally apply the checklists in
[`reviews-and-meta-analyses.md`](reviews-and-meta-analyses.md) (narrative-review thesis/
organization; Müller Table-1 items for neuroimaging meta-analyses).

## Reviewer's meta-checklist
- [ ] Focused on **major scientific points**; minor/editorial issues summarized, not enumerated.
- [ ] Organized **by section**; every specific point **numbered**.
- [ ] ~1–2 pages; each key point has a **rationale** and, where possible, an **explicit fix**.
- [ ] Judged **revision potential** (correctable vs. not), not perfection.
- [ ] Tone **honest, balanced, constructive**; review **proofed**.
- [ ] **COI declared**, **confidentiality** kept, verdict handling per journal policy.
- [ ] Delivered **on time** (or declined early).
